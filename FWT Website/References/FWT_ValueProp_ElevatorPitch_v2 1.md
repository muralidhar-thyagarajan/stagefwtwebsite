## The Race You Are Not Being Told About

[OpenAI and Anthropic have officially started the IPO race.](https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/openai-and-anthropic-have-officially-started-the-ipo-race) xAI is moving in the same direction. Meanwhile, [Bloomberg's investigation into AI circular deals](https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-ai-circular-deals/) documents how AI hardware and software companies are generating short-term revenues through each other's investment and purchase agreements — pumping valuations before going public. Elon Musk has [already engineered changes to Nasdaq index rules](https://medium.com/the-political-prism/spacex-changed-the-rules-before-its-ipo-5caea1cb92fb) to force retirement funds to absorb SpaceX stock regardless of investment merit — a [playbook that xAI, OpenAI, and Anthropic are studying closely.](https://www.theverge.com/podcast/942586/elon-musk-spacex-ipo-x-xai-index-funds)

The incentive structure of the companies whose APIs power your AI stack is increasingly oriented around one thing: maximising valuation before public markets absorb the risk.

OpenAI has missed its own internal revenue targets. Its CFO has warned that if growth does not accelerate, the company may fail to meet $1.4 trillion in infrastructure obligations across Oracle, Microsoft, and Amazon. Server-grade DRAM prices spiked 50% in 2025. Hyperscaler capital expenditure will exceed $1 trillion by 2027. Those costs will not be absorbed by investors. They will be passed to enterprise customers — at the precise moment when your workflows are embedded deeply enough that you have no credible exit.

**This is not speculation. It is the operating model.**

And yet the adoption curve continues to accelerate — because the alternative, not building AI capability at all, is equally untenable. Which means every company is now navigating between two failure modes: move too fast into vendor dependency, or move too slow and cede competitive ground.

## The Operational Risk Already in Your Stack

In March 2026, the Federal Housing Finance Agency terminated all contracts with Anthropic — overnight. Not because of poor performance or a security breach. Because of a political dispute between Anthropic and the Pentagon, two parties your institution had no relationship with and no control over. Within 72 hours, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac were scrambling to migrate workflows that had been embedded over months. The institutions with the greatest exposure were the ones who had moved fastest to adopt.

This is not an edge case. It is a preview of the category of risk that no existing vendor risk management framework was designed to handle.

**81% of enterprise leaders are now concerned about AI vendor dependency.** 47% report a key business function would stop if their primary AI vendor failed. Only 6% can switch AI vendors without material disruption. More than 80% of the $684 billion spent on enterprise AI in 2025 failed to deliver measurable business value.

### The Five Lock-in Dimensions Your Board Needs to Name

**1. Pricing lock-in.** Vendor access is currently priced below cost to drive adoption. Once your workflows are embedded and switching costs are prohibitive, pricing will reset. You will have no leverage.

**2. IP lock-in.** Who owns the model fine-tuned on your proprietary data? In most vendor contracts, the base model remains the vendor's property and the fine-tuned weights are jointly owned or vendor-retained. When the relationship ends, the institutional intelligence encoded in that model does not transfer to you.

**3. Data lock-in.** Mastercard and Plaid have both announced foundation models trained on their own transaction data — explicitly because proprietary data cannot be replicated and is the real competitive moat. When your data trains a vendor's model, you are building their moat, not yours.

**4. Regulatory lock-in.** CFPB adverse action requirements, EU AI Act Article 6, OCC Bulletin 2026-13, Fannie Mae LL-2026-04 — black-box AI from a general-purpose vendor cannot satisfy any of them. If you cannot explain the decision in plain language to a regulator or a customer, the compliance liability is yours, not the vendor's.

**5. Knowledge lock-in.** When vendor specialists build and operate your AI systems, the institutional understanding of how those systems work lives with the vendor. When the contract ends or the vendor is disrupted, that understanding leaves with them.

Regulated financial services, engineering, and technology companies are arriving at the same conclusion. The question is whether they are arriving at it before or after a vendor disruption forces their hand.
