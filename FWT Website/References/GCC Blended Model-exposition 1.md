### The GCC Blended Model — Explained Simply

Think of it as a **"strategic core + execution partner"** arrangement. Instead of choosing between fully owning an offshore team (captive GCC) or completely outsourcing work (OPD/IT services), a company does both simultaneously:

- **Your captive GCC** handles the high-value work: AI strategy, IP creation, proprietary governance, sensitive data, and domain expertise. You own it, you control it.
- **A managed services / BPO partner** handles the high-volume, standardized, repeatable work: loan processing queues, document extraction at scale, compliance screening volume. You don't own it — you contract it.

The two layers run in parallel, each doing what it's best suited for. This is also called **"bimodal sourcing"**, **"best sourcing"**, or **COPO (Company Owned, Partner Operated)**.

**The three variants** the EY article describes are a useful spectrum to place this on:

1. **Extended Office** — traditional outsourced execution center, HQ directs everything, limited autonomy. Cost play.
2. **Hybrid GCC** (the blended model) — shared ownership, the GCC handles governance and process design, a partner handles scale execution. 68% of GCC leaders currently prefer this model.
3. **Autonomous Hub** — fully owned, fully independent, end-to-end accountable. AI-first companies are heading here.

The blended model sits in the middle. It's the mainstream choice in 2026 for organizations that want strategic control without the overhead of scaling a captive center for every volume function.

### Implications for FWT — Pros and Cons

**The Pros**

The blended model is structurally good for FWT's positioning. Here's why:

It validates the BOT as the right entry point for the strategic half of the equation. ANSR's own framing — "GCC owns IP and AI strategy, managed services handles day-to-day scalable execution" — is exactly the split FWT is built for. FWT builds and transfers the strategic captive core (HITL governance, KnowledgeOps, DecisionOps, MLOps). The execution volume can be contracted to BPO providers once the client has a functioning owned center. FWT doesn't need to compete for the execution side at all.

This also expands the addressable market. A mid-size BFSI client who can't justify a 100-person captive GCC might be very comfortable with a 15-25 person AI governance and operations core (owned, via FWT) plus a Firstsource or WNS managing document processing volume (contracted). FWT's Launchpad and Growth SKUs are perfectly sized for the captive core in this model.

The 68% hybrid preference EY found confirms that the market has already moved where FWT's argument points: companies want control of the strategic layer. FWT's job is to make that layer ownable for the mid-market.

**The Cons and Risks**

The primary risk is competitive bundling. ANSR, Hexaware, Infosys BPM, and GlobalXperts all offer both sides — they can pitch "we'll build your captive core AND run your execution operations as managed services." A client who doesn't want to manage two vendor relationships may default to a single large provider, even if it means the captive ownership piece is weaker. FWT needs a clear answer to: _"Why not just do everything with Hexaware?"_

There is also a positioning confusion risk specific to FWT. The BOT model ends in full client ownership — that is its designed endpoint. But COPO and managed services models are permanent arrangements where a partner operates the center indefinitely. These look similar on the surface ("a partner is involved in my India operations") but are fundamentally different in governance terms. If clients conflate BOT-to-ownership with permanent COPO, they may not understand what FWT is actually offering, and may not see the premium value of eventual independence.

Finally, if the captive core in a blended model is small — say, 8 people doing AI governance while a BPO runs the volume — the client's board may not appreciate why that 8-person team matters enough to build through a BOT engagement rather than just adding it to the managed services contract. FWT needs to make the "even a small captive core must be owned" argument specifically for the AI governance roles, leaning on the explainability, HITL, and IP arguments in the CXO outline.

**The Strategic Takeaway for FWT**

The blended model is not a threat to FWT's core offering — it is a clarification of where FWT's offer sits and what it doesn't need to do. FWT should proactively embrace the framing: _"Build your strategic AI operations core as a captive GCC through FWT. For high-volume execution, engage whichever BPO partner you choose — that's a separate, easily comparable decision. But the governance layer, the HITL architecture, the knowledge infrastructure, the decision provenance — those must be yours. That's what we build."_

The risk is the "one-stop-shop" competitor. The response is specificity of purpose: FWT is the specialist for the layer that cannot be outsourced without regulatory and competitive consequence. That's a harder argument to win on price, and an easier argument to win on stakes.
